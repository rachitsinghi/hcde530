C5 - Data Analysis with Pandas
In answering the three interrelated analytical questions, my A5 assignment relied on the HYG v4.2 star catalog (~119,626 stars). I needed to determine if the stars can be categorized in a constellation format, whether brighter stars are the foundational structure for constellations, and finally, what are the distinguishing physical characteristics between two clusters. In the analysis notebook (a5_analysis.ipynb), all five pandas operations were applied as covered in class: df.head() and df.info() was used to learn about the data frame's dimension and column information; df.isnull().sum() showed that there were around 3,000 stars without any recorded spectral class; df[df['mag'] < 6.5] was employed to filter out all the stars not visible to the naked eye (~9,000); value_counts() was applied to the 'constellation' column to identify the areas where stars are denser, and lastly, groupby('cluster') was used thrice to calculate the mean position and number of stars in each cluster, the mean brightness, and the most frequent spectral class. The most interesting result came about from combining the analyses: when K-Means was applied to RA/Dec, it yielded 88 clusters. When I then grouped the data by clusters and summed up an is_bright flag (mag < 3.0), it was found that the vast majority of these clusters contained 2-4 bright stars, surrounded by a large number of less bright stars, indicating the likelihood that a few anchor stars determine the shape of the constellation and the others serve as fillers. One limitation that was mentioned in the wrap-up cell is that although K-Means forms clusters based on density, constellations are man-made shapes, not density-based; hence, what I have done here is merely constellation-like.

C7 - Critical Evaluation and Professional Judgment
A particularly clear instance of C7 within my project involved my initial choice of datasets for MP1. Initially, I began with three CSV files I had created (an N-body simulation with 1.2 million rows, an SDSS sky survey, and a smaller list of 29 named stars) and instructed Claude to combine them into a single document. While Claude completed this task, he also made it known that all three of the documents lacked any common key and that the 64,000 "stars" from the simulation file were merely generic particles with randomized values for their physical attributes, making it possible to place a hot blue star right next to a distant, dim red dwarf, which is physically impossible. Instead of accepting the document simply because it appeared complete, I took Claude’s statement seriously and realized that generating constellations with randomized physical properties would result in nonsensical results such as cool red stars appearing as glowing blue points. I thought about the possibility of using external data, analyzed the HYG database recommendation proposed by Claude, personally checked whether the HYG database contained the required columns (position coordinates, magnitude, B-V color, spectral type) using Sirius and Betelgeuse as examples, and made the decision to abandon the synthetic file and start all over based on the HYG database. In this case, the lesson I learned is that AI-driven tools will definitely provide you with something that looks right but isn't (a perfectly formatted CSV table with invented relationships between variables, or a description of the code rather than the question and answer).
